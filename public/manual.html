System Pipeline & Data Flow

This offline application is built around a real-time hardware-in-the-loop data pipeline. It processes physical vertical vibration events using low-cost telemetry (ESP8266 + MPU9250) and analyzes structural mechanical properties on the host machine.

MPU9250 Accelerometer
Measures physical proper vertical acceleration in the Z-Axis
↓ Samples at 500Hz
ESP8266 Microcontroller
Filters high frequency noise, checks trigger delta variance, packets raw binary batch
↓ WebSockets Transmission
Node.js Server
Applies Hamming Window, runs Cooley-Tukey Radix-2 FFT, extracts Q-Factor & Damping Ratio
↓ JSON Feeds & SQLite write
Web Dashboard & PDF Report
Displays oscilloscope/spectrum, saves structured sessions, runs AI copilot diagnostic reports

The data transmission uses a lightweight WebSockets connection. Under standby mode, the ESP8266 transmits vibration metadata updates at 1Hz. When vertical displacement triggers a vibration event, the microcontroller locks into high-speed sampling (exactly 2000µs intervals) and transmits a complete 128-point array to the host for spectral parsing.

Time-Domain Filtering

1.1 Gravity Calibration

At rest, the MPU9250 accelerometer reads gravitational acceleration of approximately \(1g\) (\(9.81\text{ m/s}^2\)). To isolate dynamic structural vibration, a baseline calibration is run on system startup, taking the arithmetic mean of 1000 rest samples:

\[ \text{Baseline} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} Z_i \]

1.2 Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

To adjust for slow sensor tilt, thermal drift, or low-frequency orientation shifts, the system continuously updates a dynamic baseline using an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) filter:

\[ MA_t = \alpha \cdot Z_t + (1 - \alpha) \cdot MA_{t-1} \]

Where the smoothing coefficient \(\alpha = 0.15\) is balanced to follow sensor tilt without absorbing high-frequency mechanical vibration pulses.

1.3 Variance-Based Jerk Detection

To run efficiently on small laptop batteries, the host computer does not calculate full FFT grids on background noise. Instead, the ESP8266 calculates standard deviation (\(\sigma\)) across a rolling window of \(N=64\) samples:

\[ \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (Z_i - MA_t)^2 \]

The active trigger threshold is set to \(1.5 \times \sigma\). When the absolute jerk value \(\Delta Z = |Z_t - MA_t|\) exceeds this threshold, the ESP8266 triggers high-speed capture.

Frequency-Domain Analysis

Analyzing vibration strictly in the time domain hides critical machine failure indicators. The system converts raw vertical displacement waveforms into frequency spectra to isolate individual rotor speeds, bearing defects, and structural resonances.

2.1 Spectral Leakage & The Hamming Window

Because the captured buffer is a finite, abrupt window of length \(N=128\), simple Fourier transformation introduces artificial high-frequency "spectral leakage" at the buffer boundaries. To eliminate this, the system pre-multiplies the raw dataset by a Hamming Window prior to processing:

\[ W(n) = 0.54 - 0.46 \cos\left( \frac{2\pi n}{N-1} \right) \] \[ Z_{\text{windowed}}[n] = Z[n] \times W(n) \]

2.2 Cooley-Tukey Radix-2 FFT

The Node.js server utilizes the Cooley-Tukey Radix-2 Decimation-in-Time Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm. By recursively splitting the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) equation into odd and even halves, execution complexity is reduced from \(O(N^2)\) to \(O(N \log N)\):

\[ X_k = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n \cdot e^{-\frac{j 2\pi k n}{N}} \]

2.3 Magnitude Scaling to Physical G-Forces

The complex output of the Radix-2 FFT (\(X_k\)) represents relative magnitude. To retrieve the **true physical amplitude in g-force** for displaying on the oscilloscope and charts, the magnitude of each frequency bin \(k\) is scaled:

\[ \text{Magnitude}_k = \sqrt{\text{Re}(X_k)^2 + \text{Im}(X_k)^2} \] \[ \text{Amplitude}_k = \frac{2}{N} \cdot \text{Magnitude}_k \]

This scaling correctly accounts for the energy distributed across positive and negative conjugate frequency pairs.

2.4 Nyquist Frequency & Bin Resolution

Based on the Nyquist sampling criterion, with a hardware timing rate of \(f_s = 500\text{Hz}\), the maximum frequency resolved is the Nyquist limit of \(250\text{Hz}\). The frequency resolution width (\(\Delta f\)) of each FFT bin is exactly:

\[ \Delta f = \frac{f_s}{N} = \frac{500}{128} \approx 3.906\text{ Hz} \]

Resonance & Damping

Resonance occurs when dynamic mechanical excitation frequencies match the structural natural frequency (\(f_n\)), resulting in severe vibration amplification and fatigue failure.

3.1 Half-Power Bandwidth Q-Factor Method

The system evaluates the sharpness of the resonance curve to calculate the dimensionless Quality Factor (Q-Factor), indicating dynamic energy storage:

  1. Identify the peak resonant amplitude \(A_{\text{max}}\) at the natural frequency \(f_n\).
  2. Compute the -3dB half-power threshold: \[ A_{\text{half}} = \frac{A_{\text{max}}}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707 \cdot A_{\text{max}} \]
  3. Linearly interpolate the frequency values to the left (\(f_1\)) and right (\(f_2\)) of the peak where amplitude cross \(A_{\text{half}}\).
  4. Calculate the Q-Factor: \[ Q = \frac{f_n}{f_2 - f_1} \]

3.2 Damping Ratio

The structural damping ratio (\(\zeta\)) represents the rate at which dynamic oscillations decay over time. For engineering structures (where \(\zeta < 0.1\)), this is calculated directly from the Q-Factor:

\[ \zeta \approx \frac{1}{2Q} \]
Damping Ratio (\(\zeta\)) Q-Factor Classification Structural Interpretation & Action
\(\zeta < 0.01\) \(Q > 50\) Critically Light Severe resonant risk. Immediate structural damping sheets or stiffening gussets required.
\(0.01 \le \zeta < 0.05\) \(10 \le Q \le 50\) Low Damping Common in welded steel structures. Avoid matching structural natural frequencies.
\(0.05 \le \zeta < 0.15\) \(3.3 \le Q < 10\) Moderate Damping Typical of composite materials, bolted frames, or elastomeric isolators. Stable.
\(\zeta \ge 0.15\) \(Q < 3.3\) High Damping Heavily cushioned foundation pads. Vibrations decay almost instantly. Safe.
